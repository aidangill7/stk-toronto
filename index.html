Three essential economic concepts, prepared with care

I

First Course

Monopolistic Competition

[WRITE YOUR MONOPOLISTIC COMPETITION ANALYSIS HERE. Define monopolistic competition: many competitors, slightly differentiated products, slight price control, low barriers to entry, high non-price competition. Explain how the demand curve sits above the MR curve, and how the market is neither allocatively nor productively efficient.]

[Connect to STK: It competes with other high-end steakhouses in Toronto — Jacob & Co., Blueblood, Black & Blue. Unlike the average monopolistic competition firm, STK has higher barriers to entry given its Yorkville location and brand. Explain how STK uses non-price competition: Instagram advertising, the DJ booth, celebrity visits.]

"STK Steakhouse uses several methods to differentiate themselves from other firms, which allows them to earn positive economic profit despite the common rule of 0 economic profit for long-run monopolistic competition firms."

[Explain excess capacity — STK could seat more people but uses MR=MC to profit maximize. Explain long-run equilibrium and how STK has managed to maintain economic profit over 10 years by sustaining differentiation.]

II

Second Course

Elasticity of Demand

[WRITE YOUR ELASTICITY ANALYSIS HERE. Define price elasticity of demand — how sensitive consumers are to price changes. Define inelastic (coefficient less than 1) vs elastic (coefficient greater than 1). Explain the characteristics of each and how luxury goods tend to be elastic.]

[Connect to STK: The goal is to be as inelastic as possible. However, STK's elasticity is heavily dependent on the economy. In a booming economy, a $10 price increase barely affects the customer base. In a downturn — like now — the same increase could significantly decrease quantity demanded.]

[Bring in Abid Chaudry's comparison of Toronto vs. Alberta — in Alberta's stronger economy, luxury goods are bought more freely, making STK-equivalent businesses more inelastic there. Discuss income elasticity: since STK is a luxury normal good, its income elasticity coefficient is greater than 1.]

Third Course

Supply, Demand & Market Equilibrium

[WRITE YOUR SUPPLY & DEMAND ANALYSIS HERE. Define supply and demand and their laws. Explain the 5 shifters of demand and the 5 shifters of supply. Explain market equilibrium — where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded.]

[Connect to STK: Explain how STK determines pricing by observing competitors at their own caliber — not The Keg. Their steaks cost $40 to source and are priced at $70–$250, averaging around $100, giving roughly a 2.5x markup. This pricing strategy reflects where supply and demand intersect for their market tier.]

[Discuss the water example: $1 cost, $10–$15 price, customers consuming 3–4 per meal. This is a prime example of price discrimination enabled by the luxury environment. Each customer unknowingly contributes significant profit through water alone. Explain how the luxury setting and brand shift the demand curve rightward — customers are willing to pay more for the same bottle of water than they would elsewhere.]
